Understanding the Neurobiology of Trauma

How the brain responds to threat, and what that means for children

What Happens in the Brain During Trauma
EarlyON Child and Family Centre Northwood (n.d.) explains that the limbic system acts as the body's internal alarm, triggering the release of stress hormones the moment danger is sensed. These hormones sharpen the senses and prepare the body to react. Once the danger passes, the body typically clears these hormones and returns to calm. Lusk (2025) adds that during a threat, the brain shifts into survival mode and temporarily shuts down the prefrontal cortex, the region responsible for reasoning and self-control, so all available energy goes toward immediate reaction.
