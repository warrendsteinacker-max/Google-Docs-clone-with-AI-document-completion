Understanding the Neurobiology of Trauma

How the brain responds to threat, and what that means for children

What Happens in the Brain During Trauma

  • EarlyON Child and Family Centre Northwood (n.d.) explains that the limbic system acts as the body's internal alarm, triggering the release of stress hormones the moment danger is sensed. These hormones sharpen the senses and prepare the body to react. Once the danger passes, the body typically clears these hormones and returns to calm.
  • Lusk (2025) adds that during a threat, the brain shifts into survival mode and temporarily shuts down the prefrontal cortex, the region responsible for reasoning and self-control, so all available energy goes toward immediate reaction.

When Trauma Becomes Chronic

  • Sorrells (2015) describes on page 31 how the brain stem rapidly processes sensory information to judge safety, and once danger is detected, it sets off a cascade of chemical and electrical signals that shift heart rate, blood pressure, and muscle tone to prepare for fight, flight, or freeze.
  • Sorrells (2015) notes on page 31 that children without chronic trauma histories return to baseline once a threat passes, but children living with ongoing neglect or abuse develop a stress system that gets stuck in the "on" position. Sorrells (2015) compares this to driving with one foot on the brake and the other on the gas, explaining on page 31 that this constant activation is especially damaging during the first five years of life, when the brain is still setting its baseline for self-regulation.

Long-Term Effects

  • EarlyON Child and Family Centre Northwood (n.d.) reports that chronically elevated stress hormones are toxic to the hippocampus, the brain structure responsible for organizing memory, while the same hormones enlarge the amygdala, making the brain increasingly sensitive to trauma-related cues over time.
  • This same source references the ACE study, which found that children with more adverse childhood experiences face significantly higher risk for serious health conditions later in life.

There Is Hope: Healing Is Possible

  • Lusk (2025) explains that therapies such as cognitive processing therapy and prolonged exposure have been shown through brain-imaging studies to rebuild the connection between the prefrontal cortex and amygdala, restoring the brain's ability to regulate its own threat response.
  • Sorrells (2015) points to a controlled study called the Hope Connection, conducted by researchers at Texas Christian University, which found that a consistent, emotionally safe, and sensory-rich environment can help guide a child's stress chemistry back toward a healthy baseline.

Key Takeaway

  • Trauma physically reshapes how the brain manages stress and safety. But the brain remains capable of change. With the right support, children can rebuild the neural pathways trauma disrupted.
